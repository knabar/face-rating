Face Rating Study 1: Debriefing Form

 

The study that you just participated in can help researchers understand the extent to which meaning is ascribed to facial features in Asian Americans:

 

  1. Do certain facial characteristics and the general "look" of Asian Americans influence how people view them? We wanted to know whether certain regions in Asia are viewed as more prototypical than others that are still classified as Asian; for example, do people see East Asians such as people of Korean, Japanese, and Chinese descent as being more Asian? What other facial characteristics most influence how people perceive the construct of race in Asian Americans: eye shape, eyeglasses, hairstyle, skin color?

 

  1. We also wanted to know whether certain phenotypic features put people at risk for being stereotyped or influences self-esteem at the individual level. Collective judgments about facial features in Asian Americans might be related to a person's racial identity and self-esteem. For example, if you judge somebody as "more" Asian because they have a more stereotypical look, how much does that influence the identity or self-esteem of the person you are observing?

 

Several studies found that certain characteristics in Asian Americans can lead to discrimination or misjudgment from others. For example, certain facial features or characteristics may be related to people's experiences of feeling more like they are part of an ingroup (Waldzus, Mummendey, & Wenzel, 2004). According to Morera, Dupont, Leyens, and Desert (2004), certain pieces of face-related information may be related to discrimination encountered by a target group.

References

Waldzus, S., Mummendey, A., & Wenzel, M. (2005). When "different" means "worse": Ingroup prototypicality in changing intergroup contexts. Journal of Experimental Social Psychology, 41, 76-83.
